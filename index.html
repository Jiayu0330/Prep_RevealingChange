Techniques to Highlight the Meaningful Changes in Data Visualizations
  • Time Series Line Chart

    • In Cairo's words, "The X-axis (horizontal) in this kind of chart represents equally spaced time intervals, and the Y-axis (vertical) corresponds to the magnitude of the variables we wish to explore or present" (P???). This kind of chart helps us find the changes of data by paying attention to the trend and seasonality.

    Example:
    This is an example of time series line charts. It shows the California's health insurance coverage from 1995 to 2010. By plotting the health insurance coverage in each year, we can see the change of health insurance coverage, which goes up after 2000 and stay constant. Annotations in specific years help us better understand the change.

  • Seasonal Subseries Chart

    • It is a powerful tool for detecting seasonality in a time series. However, we need to know the period of the seasonality to plot the chart. Monthly data is good because it has a period of 12. We can see the change of data clearly by looking at the difference between group and within group.

    Example:
    This is an example of seasonal subseries charts. It is about the sales trends by year within each month. In this chart, we can easily compare the sales of each month. The black lines show the trend in performance for each moth, while the gray horizontal lines show the average sales for each month.

  • Zero-based Indexed Chart

    • Using zero-based indexed chart can tell us the difference between data and the index origin.
    Percentage change = ((Each score – Index origin) / Indexorigin) × 100

  • Horizon Chart

    • It is useful when you need to "display dozens of line charts simultaneously" (P???).
    Three steps to make a horizon chart:
    (1) subdivide its vertical scale evenly, and color code positive and negative values,
    (2) mirror the color bands corresponding to negative values, and then
    (3) collapse the color bands. Following these steps results in a much shorter chart.
